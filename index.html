AI-SDLC · DevOps Vertical Input

DevOps support for AI-SDLC at Dhwani RIS

The AI-SDLC has three personas — Product Builder, Architect, Account Manager — and ten SDLC steps. This is the DevOps vertical's response: how DevOps plugs into every one of those steps, supports every persona, and turns AI-assisted delivery into Dhwani's delivery edge.

Prepared by Dhwani RIS DevOps For AI-SDLC closing sessions Status Draft v0.2 · 2026-05-14

00 Where this document fits

On 7 May, the AI-SDLC session at Dhwani agreed three personas and a per-step ownership table across the ten SDLC steps. The closing sessions on 12 May and 14 May are locking the personas, the per-step accountability, and the project-start enablers (stack, tooling, sign-off, AI tooling, pilot).

Each vertical was asked to come in carrying its own inputs — "as the voice of the vertical, not just yourself." This document is the DevOps vertical's input.

DevOps is the connective layer of the AI-SDLC. The personas define who does the work, the 10 steps define what the work is. DevOps defines how the work flows — the pipelines, environments, guardrails, evidence, and observability that make AI-assisted delivery fast and accountable, end to end.

01 The three personas (recap)

Recapped here so this document is self-contained. To be locked in the closing sessions.

PB

Product Builder

Empowered with AI to ship. Owns Requirements, Mock-ups, Tech-Spec drafting, and the Dev + QA loop execution. The high-leverage role — most of DevOps' value lands here.

AR

Architect

Defines guardrails. Owns architectural integrity and sign-off. Prevents drift across projects. Reviewer and decision-maker, not implementer.

AM

Account Manager

Owns the client relationship and the intent. Holds scope at Intent Check, drives Client UAT, owns Go-Live handover.

03 Per-step DevOps support

For each of the ten steps: what happens, who owns it, where DevOps plugs in, and what this unlocks. The order and naming match the per-step accountability table agreed on 7 May.

01

Intent Check

AM + PB

What happens

The AM holds the client's intent; together with the PB they translate it into a scoped engagement.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • Published delivery-posture catalogue — environment tiers, observability commitments, security baselines — that the AM can quote at Intent.
  • Standard SLA / SLO bands the AM can promise without checking back with Engineering.
  • Estimating shortcuts: tier-by-tier indicative timelines and cost ranges.

What this unlocks

The AM enters Intent conversations with a credible delivery posture in hand — fewer surprises later, faster scope agreement now.

02

Requirement Gathering

PB

What happens

The PB captures and refines requirements with AI assistance.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • Templated requirement format that CI can read — a PR linked to a requirement ID lets the pipeline assert "this change was for that ask".
  • Audit trail starts at the requirement, not the commit.
  • Repo scaffolding ships with the requirement-link convention pre-wired.

What this unlocks

Any line of code at Go-Live can be traced back to the business need it served. The AM and Architect both gain provenance for free.

03

Mock-up First

PB · client sign-off

What happens

The PB builds mock-ups with AI and gets client sign-off before any production code is written.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • Hosted preview URL per mock-up branch. Share a working link with the client, not a screenshot deck.
  • Auto-teardown after sign-off or 7-day idle — cost predictable.
  • Standard mock-up sign-off captured against the PR — the audit trail records who approved which mock-up, and when.

What this unlocks

Clients sign off on something interactive. Sign-offs are auditable artefacts, not email threads.

04

Tech Spec

PB drafts · Architect signs off

What happens

The PB drafts the tech spec (AI-assisted). The Architect signs off — reviewer and decision-maker, not implementer.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • ADR template ships in every new repo.
  • Reference-architecture and IaC catalogue published. The Architect signs against a known catalogue, not from scratch.
  • Automated check: the tech-spec PR must link the chosen reference pattern (or explain the deviation).
  • Architect → PB escalation runs through GitHub PR review with required-reviewer rules.

What this unlocks

The Architect scales across many more projects without becoming the bottleneck. AI-suggested designs are sanity-checked against Dhwani's chosen patterns, every time.

05

Dev Plan + Test Cases

PB + QA

What happens

PB and QA plan the development and test coverage.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • Test scaffolds and CI pipeline templates ship with the repo.
  • Coverage thresholds and required test types defined per project tier.
  • Test-data anonymisation pipeline available — QA can request anonymised data slices for new test cases.

What this unlocks

The team doesn't reinvent test setup per project. QA plans against a known harness.

06

Dev + QA Loop

Engineering + QA High-leverage

What happens

Code is written, with AI assistance, by the PB and engineers. The most dynamic loop — and where AI volume is highest.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • AI-aware code guardrails: secrets scan, "hallucination check" (do the imports actually exist?), test-quality gate (no mock-only tests), ai-generated PR label.
  • Pre-commit baseline rolled out to every active repo — issues caught at the laptop.
  • Self-service review environments in under 15 minutes — no waiting on DevOps.
  • Conventional commits enforced. Audit trail stays machine-readable.

What this unlocks

AI-assisted code becomes safer than purely human-written code. Volume goes up; the bar holds.

07

Client UAT

AM + Client

What happens

The AM walks the client through a UAT environment and collects sign-off.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • Dedicated UAT-tier environment, provisioned on request, prod-shaped.
  • Anonymised production-like data seeded; no PII leaves prod.
  • Per-project status page — the client can see env health independently.
  • UAT sign-off captured in the PR / release record. The AM never says "let me check with DevOps".

What this unlocks

The AM shares a working URL, not a deck. UAT sign-offs are durable artefacts.

08

Pre-Prod Setup

Engineering High-leverage

What happens

Engineering prepares a production-shape environment for the release.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • IaC is templated — Pre-Prod is provisioned from the same modules as Prod, only smaller / segregated.
  • Secrets injected from Vault. Never in env files.
  • Observability is default-on — dashboards, alerts, runbook links present before deploy, not after.
  • Database backup and restore policy configured before first deploy.

What this unlocks

No project ships "blind". Pre-Prod is a faithful rehearsal for Prod, not a guess.

09

Perf + Security

Engineering + Security High-leverage

What happens

Load tests, performance baseline, security review.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • Kotwal scan — Dhwani's own scanner — runs CERT tests, penetration tests, and open-source vulnerability checks as a required gate.
  • Load test runs in a dedicated environment tier; performance regression compared against the last release.
  • Evidence packet for the Security sign-off assembled automatically (scan reports, perf baseline, exception log).

What this unlocks

Security signs off against evidence, not assurances. Performance regressions are caught here, not after Go-Live.

10

Go-Live

Engineering + AM High-leverage

What happens

Promotion to production. AM owns client handover.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • Release-readiness portal — promote-to-production is enabled only when every required check is green (or has a signed exception).
  • A versioned Release Readiness Report is published. The AM hands it to the client.
  • Post-deploy monitoring tightens for the first 30 minutes — rollback triggers documented in advance.
  • Rollback rehearsed; runbook linked from the report.

What this unlocks

Predictable, defensible Go-Lives. The AM shares evidence with the client, not assurances. Hot-fix releases drop sharply.

+

Beyond Go-Live · Operate & Feedback

Engineering + DevOps

What happens

The application runs in production. Incidents, usage patterns, and client signals flow back into the next Intent Check. The lifecycle is a loop.

Where DevOps plugs in

  • Default observability stack on every project — metrics, logs, traces, runtime health.
  • Mukhbir — Dhwani's own monitoring — tracks uptime, SSL, security headers, response health, and produces shareable runtime reports.
  • Weekly + monthly automated reports to project owners — uptime, performance, errors, infrastructure usage, security signals, cost.
  • Blameless incident review template for every Sev-1 / Sev-2.
  • Quarterly portfolio review feeds findings back to the AM and PB for the next intent.

What this unlocks

The 10 steps are not where AI-SDLC ends. Operations and feedback close the loop — each cycle, the bar gets higher. (Recommended addition to the official table.)

04 Project-start enablers — what DevOps brings

The closing sessions are asking each vertical for inputs against four enablers: stack & tooling, sign-off & governance, AI in the SDLC, and rollout. Here is the DevOps input on each.

Stack & tooling

A maintained reference stack and project scaffold per repo type.

  • Starter repos with pre-commit baseline, CI/CD template, IaC modules, and observability stubs already wired.
  • Component-library catalogue published; PBs pick from a shelf, they don't shop the web.
  • Mock-up tooling integrated with the hosted preview environment workflow.

Sign-off & governance

Every sign-off is an artefact, not an email.

  • Standard tech-spec doc set (DevOps + Architect to co-design): inputs, outputs, dependencies, deployment shape, observability plan.
  • Mock-up sign-off, UAT sign-off, Security sign-off, AM handover — all captured against the PR / release record.
  • Architect ↔ PB escalation path runs through required-reviewer rules. No out-of-band approvals.

AI in the SDLC

Quality gates apply regardless of who or what wrote the code.

  • AI-aware gates: suspicion gate, hallucination check, test-quality gate, dependency justification, ai-generated labels.
  • Same gates apply to AI-augmented work and human-only work — no special casing, no exemptions.
  • DevOps does not prescribe which AI assistant each persona uses — DevOps prescribes the bar the output must clear.

Rollout

Pilot project criteria + sequencing.

  • Pilot criteria: not client-critical from day one; exercises every one of the 10 steps; PM and Tech Lead willing to be the pilot.
  • Sequence by repo tier — Tier S (review envs) first, Tier M (UAT) next, Tier L (perf) last.
  • Parallel initiatives folded in: the existing pre-commit work, IaC modules, and Kotwal / Mukhbir integrations are foundation, not duplication.

05 How it lands for each persona

The same DevOps layer, lived from three different chairs.

Product Builder

"I capture the requirement, draft the mock-up, draft the tech spec, then run the Dev + QA loop with AI assistance. DevOps gives me the pipeline the moment the repo is created — guardrails on AI-generated code, automatic review environments per branch, test scaffolds ready to fill. I move fast without becoming the project's risk."

Architect

"My job is to sign off, not to implement. DevOps publishes reference IaC and architecture patterns. When a Product Builder submits a tech spec, my review is anchored on whether they used the catalogue — and policy-as-code blocks the drift patterns I'd otherwise catch by hand. I scale across many more projects without being the bottleneck."

Account Manager

"At Intent Check I quote a delivery posture I can stand behind, because DevOps has already defined what Tier 2 environments and SLAs look like. At UAT I share a working URL, not a slide. At Go-Live I share a published Release Readiness Report. The client sees evidence, not assurances."

Client

"Every release comes with a one-page readiness report. Every month I get an automated health report — uptime, performance, anything to watch. I always know what's being delivered and how it's performing. I never have to chase."

07 Roadmap

Four quarters. Each phase delivers complete DevOps support for a contiguous slice of the 10 steps. Leadership reviews at each phase boundary.

  1. Phase 0 · Now

    Foundation · all steps

    Inventory current tooling step by step. Pick the pilot project against the criteria in §04. Lock the project-start enabler answers. Define "done" for each phase exit with senior leadership.

  2. Phase 1 · Q1

    Steps 2–6 · Build the spine

    Requirement-link convention, ADR template, reference IaC catalogue, AI-aware code guardrails, pre-commit baseline. The PB has a working spine end-to-end. First metric: fewer issues reaching review.

  3. Phase 2 · Q2

    Steps 3, 6, 7 · Environments self-service

    Self-service portal MVP for one pilot. Three environment tiers operational. Mock-up preview URLs flow automatically. Anonymised data nightly. First metric: time-to-environment under 15 minutes.

  4. Phase 3 · Q3

    Steps 8–10 · Release with evidence

    Release-readiness portal live. Kotwal integrated as a required gate. Policy-as-code wired. Every release ships a Release Readiness Report. First metric: 100% of releases through the gate.

  5. Phase 4 · Q4

    Beyond · Operate & Feedback

    Default observability stack on every active project. Mukhbir feeding status pages and auto-reports. Quarterly portfolio review formalised. First metric: 100% of projects observed by default.

08 What we need from leadership

Three categories of support. The decisions below align to the project-start enabler questions surfaced in the 7 May session.

People

One to two dedicated DevOps engineers, for four quarters.

Plus part-time support from each project's Tech Lead during the rollout window for that project. No new permanent hires required beyond what's already planned.

Tooling

A small set of licences for security scanning and observability.

The bulk of the stack is open source. Our internal tools — Kotwal and Mukhbir — anchor the security and runtime layers. Detailed line-item in the implementation plan.

Decisions

Standards calls only senior leadership can make.

  • Front-end stack standardisation — Vue / React / both?
  • One CI/CD platform across all projects, or accept mixed tooling?
  • Cloud target — AWS primary, with on-prem for government projects?
  • Internal portal — build (Backstage) or buy (Port / Cortex)?
  • Who classifies project criticality tiers — PM, Tech Lead, or DevOps?

09 Open questions for the closing sessions

To carry into the 12 May and 14 May sessions, framed from the DevOps vertical.

  1. Does the official 10-step list close at Go-Live, or do we explicitly add an 11th step for Operate & Feedback? (DevOps recommends the latter — see §03 +Beyond.)
  2. Multi-persona handling — when one person plays PB and AM (small projects), which sign-offs must still be separated?
  3. Pilot project — which candidate exercises all 10 steps without being client-critical from day one?
  4. AI tooling per persona — does DevOps prescribe the toolchain or only the gates the output must clear? (DevOps recommends the latter.)
  5. Exception process for failing pre-Go-Live checks — who signs, and how is the exception audited?
  6. Architect ↔ PB escalation — required-reviewer in GitHub PRs, or a separate forum?
  7. Government-project specifics — data residency per state and per scheme?
  8. Phase-exit definition of "done" — who signs each phase off in the roadmap?
