Strategic Plan · For Senior Leadership

The Future of Delivery at Dhwani RIS

Code now ships from PMs, analysts, and developers alike — all assisted by AI. This is the plan for how DevOps turns that shift into Dhwani's biggest delivery advantage: safer, faster, and more confident than ever before.

Prepared by Dhwani RIS DevOps Audience Senior Leadership Decision needed by Q3 2026 Version Draft v0.1 · 2026-05-14

01 Why now

The way software gets built at Dhwani RIS has fundamentally changed. Until recently, "writing code" meant a developer at a keyboard. Today, AI assistants help everyone contribute — a PM drafting a small flow, an analyst tightening a report, a developer scaffolding a service. That is a tremendous opportunity, and it is already happening on our projects.

It also surfaces a question senior leadership needs to answer: what does our DevOps function look like when anyone can ship code? The old answer — pre-commit hooks, linters, and a manual checklist — was designed for a world where developers wrote and reviewed each other's code. It does not match how AI fails, and it does not scale to the number of contributors AI unlocks.

The opportunity in one line. AI lets us deliver more, with more people. The right DevOps support makes sure "more" never means "less safe", and gives senior leadership a clear, evidence-based picture of every project's health.

02 The shift we're navigating

This is the journey, in one view. The right column is where we're going, with DevOps support designed for the AI-SDLC era.

Today

  • Only developers write code.
  • Reviews focus on style and syntax.
  • Environments take days to provision.
  • Release readiness is a verbal assurance.
  • Issues are discovered in production.
  • Stakeholders ask for status. Teams scramble to produce it.

With AI-SDLC + DevOps Support

  • Anyone — PM, analyst, developer — contributes safely.
  • Reviews focus on intent and behaviour, not just style.
  • Environments are on-demand in under 15 minutes.
  • Every release ships with a published readiness report.
  • Issues are caught and prevented before clients see them.
  • Stakeholders get health reports automatically — weekly or monthly.

C1 Smart Code Guardrails

AI-assisted code becomes safer, not riskier, than purely human-written code.

What changes

  • Secrets, weak security patterns, and risky shortcuts are caught at the contributor's laptop — before any push to a shared branch.
  • Code that "looks right but is wrong" — generic patterns, fabricated APIs, tests that pass without exercising real behaviour — is flagged automatically.
  • Imported libraries and functions are verified to actually exist (a common AI failure mode).
  • AI-assisted contributions are labelled clearly, so human reviewers know what to scrutinise twice.

What stakeholders see

  • Far fewer security and compliance issues making it past code review.
  • A measurable, repeatable bar for what "good code" means at Dhwani.
  • Reviewer time spent on logic and design, not on chasing formatting issues.

C2 On-Demand Environments

Any team member can spin up a working environment in under fifteen minutes — without waiting on DevOps.

What changes

  • A simple self-service portal: pick the project, the branch, and the purpose. A working URL comes back in minutes.
  • Three sizes of environment — for quick reviews, full UAT, and performance testing — so people pick the right one for the job.
  • Idle environments are torn down automatically. Costs stay predictable.
  • Environments are seeded with safe, anonymised data. No client PII ever leaves production.

What stakeholders see

  • Demos to clients the same day a feature is built.
  • Teams unblocked from each other — parallel work instead of queueing.
  • Environment costs that are visible, predictable, and tied to project tiers.

C3 Confident Releases

No release goes live until a published readiness checklist is green. Every release ships with a one-page report.

What changes

Each release passes through a release-readiness portal. The portal runs the checks, gathers the evidence, and only enables Promote to Production when every required box is green (or has a signed, recorded exception).

01
Security scans pass

Anchored on Kotwal, our own scanner — covering open-source vulnerabilities and penetration tests. Zero criticals, zero highs.

02
End-to-end tests pass

Every critical user journey is exercised before release.

03
Load and performance hold up

The application meets its performance targets at expected peak load.

04
No performance regression

Less than 10% slower than the previous release.

05
Backup and restore drill complete

The team has rehearsed restoring from backup in the last 30 days.

06
Observability ready

Dashboards, alerts, and a runbook are in place before — not after — release.

07
Data protection verified

PII is encrypted at rest and masked in non-production environments.

08
Compliance verified

For government projects, audit logging and retention policies are confirmed live.

09
Rollback plan documented

If something goes wrong, the team knows exactly how to revert.

10
Sign-offs collected

Tech Lead, QA, DevOps — and Security for sensitive projects — have signed.

What stakeholders see

  • A consistent, published bar for "ready to ship" that every project follows.
  • A versioned Release Readiness Report — shareable with clients, archived for audit.
  • Far fewer hot-fix releases right after a deploy. Far fewer surprise client calls.

C4 Always-On Visibility

Every project is observed by default. Reports go to stakeholders without anyone having to ask.

What changes

  • A default monitoring stack is applied to every new project. No project ships "blind".
  • Runtime health is tracked by Mukhbir, our own monitoring tool — uptime, SSL, security headers, response health.
  • Weekly and monthly automated reports cover uptime, performance, errors, infrastructure usage, security signals, and (for cloud projects) cost.
  • Each project has a status page — clients can see system health themselves.
  • Alerts reach the right people in Slack and on pager. Issues surface before clients notice.

What stakeholders see

  • A single-pane view of project health across the portfolio.
  • Early warning when a project starts to drift in quality, performance, or cost.
  • Hard numbers to back up infrastructure, scaling, and renewal decisions.

04 A day in the future

What does this future actually feel like, for the people in it? Four short scenes.

For a PM

"I describe a small feature in plain English. The AI drafts it. A review environment is ready within minutes, with safe sample data. I demo it to the client the same afternoon — and have a clear answer when they ask, 'Is this safe to roll out?'"

For an analyst

"I propose a small change to a report. Automated checks confirm it's safe and won't break anything else. A developer reviews the logic. It's live within the week, not the month. I never have to ask DevOps to 'set something up' for me."

For a developer

"I focus on the hard problems — architecture, edge cases, integrations. The repetitive scaffolding is handled by AI, and the guardrails catch its mistakes before they reach me. I spend more time on review and design, less on babysitting boilerplate."

For a client

"Every release comes with a one-page readiness report. Every month I get an automated health report — uptime, performance, anything to watch. I always know what's being delivered and how it's performing. I never have to chase."

06 Roadmap

Four quarters. Each quarter delivers a complete, visible capability — not a half-built tool. Leadership reviews at the end of each phase before continuing.

  1. Phase 0 · Now

    Foundation

    Inventory current tooling. Classify projects by criticality. Agree the definition of "done" with senior leadership. Pick the pilot project.

  2. Phase 1 · Q1

    Smart Code Guardrails

    AI-aware code checks live in CI. Kotwal integrated as a required step. The pre-commit baseline reaches every active repo. First metric: fewer issues reaching code review.

  3. Phase 2 · Q2

    On-Demand Environments

    Self-service portal MVP live for one pilot project. Three environment tiers operational. Anonymised data snapshots flowing nightly. First metric: time-to-environment under fifteen minutes.

  4. Phase 3 · Q3

    Confident Releases

    Release-readiness portal live. All ten checks wired up. First release passes through the gate end-to-end. First metric: every production release has a published readiness report.

  5. Phase 4 · Q4

    Always-On Visibility

    Default observability stack applied to every active project. Auto-reports going out weekly and monthly. Public-or-private status pages live per project. First metric: 100% of projects observed by default.

07 What we need from leadership

To make this real on the timeline above, three categories of support are needed.

People

One to two dedicated DevOps engineers, for four quarters.

Plus part-time support from each project's Tech Lead during the rollout window for that project. No new permanent hires required beyond what's already planned.

Tooling

A small set of licences for security scanning and observability.

The bulk of the stack is open source. Our internal tools (Kotwal, Mukhbir) anchor the security and runtime layers. A detailed line-item appears in the implementation plan.

Decisions

A handful of standards calls only senior leadership can make.

  • Standardise on one CI/CD platform across all projects, or accept mixed tooling?
  • Cloud target — AWS primary, with on-prem for government projects?
  • Build the team portal ourselves, or buy a ready platform?
  • Who classifies project criticality tiers — PM, Tech Lead, or DevOps?

08 Open questions for the team

These do not need to be answered by leadership — but they need to be answered before Phase 1 closes. They are tracked alongside this plan.

  1. What is the right pilot project — one that exercises all four capabilities without being client-critical from day one?
  2. Exception process — who signs exceptions on failing pre-go-live checks, and how are those signs-off audited?
  3. Government-project specifics — data residency requirements per state and per scheme?
  4. Internal portal — does Backstage clear our needs, or do we evaluate a paid platform?
  5. Kotwal's API and CLI surface for full CI integration — is it ready, or does Phase 1 include a small extension?
  6. Project tiering — who owns the classification day-to-day, and how often is it reviewed?
  7. What does success look like at each phase exit, and who signs it off?
